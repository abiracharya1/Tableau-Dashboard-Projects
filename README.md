## 📁 Projects

### 📊 Project 1 — NIH Healthcare Funding Analysis

**Files:** `Tableau-Project-1(HealthCare Analysis).pdf` · `Healthcare_Analysis_Presentation.pptx` · `Project-1-Datasets(RCDC Summary).xlsx`

#### Business Problem
NIH allocates billions in research funding annually across hundreds of disease categories. Policymakers and researchers needed a visual framework to understand whether funding levels align with disease burden — and which conditions remain chronically underfunded relative to mortality impact.

#### Dataset
- **RCDC (Research, Condition, and Disease Categorization) Summary data**
- Coverage: **300+ disease areas, 2008–2024**
- Fields: Disease category, funding amount by year, estimated mortality, research activity

#### Analysis Performed
- NIH funding trend analysis from 2008 to 2024 by disease category
- Funding vs mortality correlation across disease areas
- Year-on-year funding growth and decline identification
- Underfunded disease category identification
- Research prioritisation analysis for policy support

#### Dashboard Features
- Interactive filters by disease category and year range
- Funding vs mortality scatter plot with outlier callouts
- Time-series trend lines by disease area
- Ranked view of top-funded and bottom-funded conditions
- KPI summary for total funding and coverage

#### Key Insights
- Significant funding imbalance exists — several high-mortality conditions receive disproportionately low research investment
- Funding trends show sustained growth in a few categories (e.g. cancer, COVID-19) while others remain flat
- Correlation analysis reveals disease areas where funding does not track mortality burden
- Underfunded categories represent clear opportunities for research prioritisation

#### Business / Policy Recommendations
- Redirect a portion of funding toward high-mortality, low-investment disease areas
- Use mortality-adjusted funding ratios as a standard benchmark for annual allocation reviews
- Track funding momentum year-on-year to identify emerging vs declining research priorities
